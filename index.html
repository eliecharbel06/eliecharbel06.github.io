01Data analysis · Python · solo

Fuel prices, and what they were hiding

My work: I did the analysis and wrote the report myself · Built with: Python, pandas, Matplotlib

A fuel-retail strategy team wanted to understand why petrol costs so wildly different depending on where and when you fill up. I took three months of NSW FuelCheck data, cleaned it into something trustworthy, and let the patterns surface.

The headline still surprises me: more than a 120 cent-per-litre gap between the cheapest and most expensive suburbs, and a price line that quietly slumped in February before climbing off a cliff through March. None of that shows up in a single average, you only see it once you cut the data by place, time, brand and fuel type.

“Average prices alone do not tell the full story. A smaller number of unusually high prices are significant enough to influence the overall mean.” My words, from the report

What I actually did

  • Wrangled the data: merged monthly Excel/CSV files, killed duplicates, standardised suburb, brand and fuel-type fields, fixed types and missing values
  • Asked the data questions: group-by analysis across suburb, brand, fuel type and time
  • Made it readable: line, bar, histogram and box plots, each tied to a real business question
  • Turned it into advice: pricing and positioning recommendations a strategy team could act on

02Cybersecurity · governance · solo

When a car maker grinds to a halt

Solo report · reflecting on a consulting pitch · Focus: the Jaguar Land Rover incident

For a cybersecurity consulting pitch, I took the Jaguar Land Rover disruption and argued it wasn't really a "hacking" story, it was a governance story. The damage came from unclear data ownership and weak incident preparedness, not just a clever attacker.

My recommendation was to stand up a Data Governance Office, and I worked hard to make the idea land with a non-technical audience, including comparing business continuity to learning how to fall safely when bouldering. The report that followed is an honest reflection on my role, the team, and the management thinking behind it.

“I aimed to present JLR's disruption as a failure of organisational resilience, data ownership and incident preparedness, rather than just a technical attack.” My words, from the report

What it shows about me

  • I think in systems: data governance, organisational resilience, incident preparedness
  • I know the frameworks: risk management, zero trust, business continuity, ethics
  • I can translate: turning technical risk into something a boardroom understands
  • I reflect honestly: a candid SWOT of my own and the team's performance

03Cybersecurity · solo

Securing the corner café

Solo assessment · a fictional consultancy, "SecureStart" · Focus: tiny food-service businesses

Small businesses are an easy target and rarely have anyone watching their back. I invented a consultancy, SecureStart, and walked through three real-world-style food-service microbusinesses to map how their networks actually look and where they break.

The fun part was getting concrete: edge routers, POS tablets, guest Wi-Fi, kitchen printers, the lot, then tying every recommendation back to frameworks that matter here in Australia: the ACSC Essential Eight, the Privacy Act 1988, and PCI-DSS.

“SecureStart is dedicated to helping Australian small businesses find weaknesses, strengthen cyber resilience and align with local frameworks like the ACSC Essential Eight, Privacy Act 1988 and PCI-DSS.” My words, from the intro

What it shows about me

  • I can read a network: mapping edge, distribution, wireless, POS, back-office and IoT layers
  • I spot the weak points: common vulnerabilities across comparable businesses

04Cybersecurity · incident response · solo

After the breach: a plan for executives

Solo report · standards-aligned · Focus: the Western Sydney University breaches, 2021–2025

I took a string of real incidents at Western Sydney University and turned them into something a small-business executive could actually use, a calm, high-level incident-response plan written for the boardroom, not the server room.

It maps the failures to ASD Essential Eight, NIST CSF 2.0, ISO 27001/27035 and APP 11, traces the root causes, MFA gaps, weak identity governance, supplier exposure, slow detection, and lays out both immediate and long-term moves.

“A high-level, standards-aligned incident response plan… to reduce credential compromise, shorten detection latency and bolster operational resilience.” My words, from the executive summary

What it shows about me

  • I map to standards: Essential Eight, NIST CSF 2.0, ISO 27001/27035, APP 11
  • I find the root cause: identity governance, MFA, supplier risk, detection latency
  • I write for the reader: management-level, action-first, no jargon for its own sake

Modelling the railway

Solo assessment · data modelling · Focus: the Rail Freight Service Australia case

Before you can build a database, you have to truly understand the business. For the Rail Freight Service Australia case I modelled two tightly connected areas, how customers and their commercial bookings are structured, and how those bookings turn into real freight movements.

The interesting wrinkle was customer arrangements: standard, contract and project-based clients all behave differently, and the model had to hold that without falling apart.

“RFSA must manage different types of customers and customer arrangements, whilst also recording bookings and the actual freight movements used to fulfil those bookings.” My words, from the overview

07Business analysis · solo

Solar power, and who gets a say

My work: the business-analysis sections · Built around: a real coal-to-solar project in Mongolia

In Ulaanbaatar's ger districts, households still heat with coal, and a clean-energy transition quietly risked leaving women out, since they manage household energy but often lack financial access or formal recognition. I applied business-analysis tools to design a socio-technical system that puts them back in the decision.

The work runs from enterprise and environment analysis through current/future process modelling to a product vision, roadmap and Agile delivery, framed against SDG 5 and SDG 7.

“The success of the coal-to-solar initiative really depends not only on the technology supplied, but on whether women are financially included, digitally confident and formally recognised in community energy roles.” My words, from the report

What it shows about me

  • I analyse the whole system: enterprise and external-environment analysis, problem framing
  • I model processes: current vs. future state with enterprise modelling techniques
  • I plan delivery: product vision, roadmap and Agile Scrum
  • I keep people in frame: a gender-inclusive lens tied to the SDGs

Personas that earn trust

My part: refining the user personas & tying them to design decisions · Project: GreenProof, a greenwashing-verification platform

GreenProof helps people check whether a fashion brand's sustainability claims are real. During development I owned two user personas, and learned the hard way that a persona is useless until it actually drives a design decision.

Team feedback flagged that my drafts described motivations well but didn't connect to the product, and our credibility system still "felt like guesswork." Fixing that turned vague opinions into testable hypotheses: the moment the project clicked for me.

“Our discussion on GreenProof's trust display really shifted from opinion-based to actual testable hypotheses.” My words, from the reflection

What it shows about me

  • I push for rigour: replacing gut-feel with hypotheses you can test
